Abstract
Context: Software quality is undergoing a profound transformation, driven by
state-of-the-art research on the application of emerging technologies in software
development processes. Specifically, the use of generative Artificial Intelligence
(AI) may represent an opportunity to advance the state of practice in this
domain. Objectives: To assess the industrial readiness and availability of Gen-
erative AI-based solutions for software quality, classifying them according to
ISO/IEC25010attributesandSDLCphases.Method:Anempiricalassessment
ofthestateofpracticewasconducted,employingaRapidMultivocalLiterature
Review(RMLR)protocolasadatacollectioninstrumenttoscreenevidencefrom

Code). Results: We identified 24 potentially usable solutions. However, the
analysisrevealsalowtechnologicalmaturity,withmostsolutionsbeingacademic
prototypes hampered by fundamental technical limitations and adoption chal-
lenges.Theseincludethe“lastmileproblem”intranslatingresearchprototypes
into reliable, production-ready tools; the “strategic adoption dilemma” forc-
ing practitioners to trade off between proprietary lock-in and high open-source

1 Introduction
SoftwareEngineering(SE)hasundergoneprofoundtransformationsinrecentdecades,
drivenbyemergingtechnologies,witharecentemphasisonGenerativeArtificialIntel-
ligence (AI), whose potential to impact software quality processes significantly has
been widely researched. Recent studies have described expectations of significant pro-
ductivitygainsacrossvariousstagesoftheSoftwareDevelopmentLifeCycle(SDLC),
driven by these new technologies. Specifically in software quality, there is growing
adoptionofGenerativeAI-basedsolutionsforSEactivitiessuchasvulnerabilitydetec-
tion (Kalouptsoglou et al. 2026, 2025), test generation and automation (Alagarsamy
et al. 2025; Tang et al. 2024), defect localization (Qin et al. 2025; Xu et al. 2026),
codeverificationandsecurity(Fakhouryetal.2024;Washizaki2024).Thesesolutions
havebeenrecognizedfortheirabilitytoreducehumanintervention,accelerateproject
schedules, decrease manual effort, shorten development time, and consequently con-
tribute to improving software quality (Hou et al. 2024; Banh et al. 2025; Raghi et al.
2024).
However, software quality is a multifaceted concept. To assess the potential effec-
tivenessofGenAIinthesesolutions,itisimportanttodefinequalitybeyondsubjective
metrics and align it with standardized models that subdivide quality into specific
characteristics, such as functional suitability, reliability, performance efficiency, secu-
rity, and maintainability (ISO/IEC 2011). This model type provides researchers and
practitioners with a systematic approach to benchmarking and evaluating software
products based on clearly defined criteria (Rafi et al. 2025).
Despitethesepotentialadvantages,adoptingGenerativeAI-basedsolutionsinSE,
especiallythosebasedonlargelanguagemodels(LLMs),hasprovencomplexandchal-
lenging.Amongthem,issuesrelatedtoethics,security,privacy,dependenceonexternal
vendors, and the potential biases inherent to the AI models stand out (Russo 2024;
Terragni et al. 2025). Moreover, the effective adoption of these solutions depends on
developmentteams’abilitytointegratethesetechnologiesintotheirworkflows(Russo
2024).Beyondthesecross-cuttingchallenges,priorresearchhasalsoreportedsolution-
specific technical limitations affecting real-world adoption, including issues related to
robustness across heterogeneous software projects, high rate of false positives, limited

systemic characteristic of the field. For this study, we operationalize the key concepts
of the research question as follows:
• Availability: Public accessibility of the solution artifacts, such as source code,
binaries, or access via internet-based platforms, combined with sufficient docu-
mentation to allow independent exploration, replication, and evaluation by third
parties.
• IndustrialReadiness:Degreeoftechnologicalmaturityandoperationalstability
requiredforportabilityanduseinproductionenvironments.Thisencompassesnot
only robustness and scalability but also the identification of potential limitations
and risks that could hinder industrial adoption.
Given this context, this study aims to answer the following research question:
RQ1: What is the current state of industrial readiness and availability of Gen-
erative AI-based academic and commercial solutions to support software quality
activities in Software Engineering?
To answer this question, we conducted an empirical assessment of the state of prac-
tice. We employed a Rapid Multivocal Literature Review (RMLR) protocol as a data
collection instrument to integrate evidence from both scientific and grey literature.
Thisapproachfocusesonidentifying,coding,andanalyzingGenerativeAI-basedsolu-
tions that are currently supporting or have clear potential for practical application in
software quality activities.
Finally, the concept of “Generative AI-based solution” adopted in this study is
broad regarding artifact types (e.g., tools, frameworks, applications), but strictly
defined in terms of the underlying technology. Our focus is on solutions capable of
producing or synthesizing new content associated with software quality activities.
Consequently, approaches relying exclusively on predictive or discriminative models
(typicallyencoder-onlyarchitecturessuchastheBERTfamily)forclassificationtasks
are excluded from this study. This distinction ensures that the evaluation addresses
the specific limitations and maturity challenges inherent to generative technologies,
suchasnon-determinismandhallucinationrisks,distinguishingthemfromestablished
predictive paradigms.

Several studies have investigated the role of Generative AI in Software Engineering

3 Methodology
This study aligns with recent investigations in software quality that use systematic
review protocols as a rigorous diagnostic tool to establish the state of the art before
proposing technical advancements or guidelines (Ahmad and Kuo 2025). However,
given our specific objective of assessing industrial readiness rather than relying solely
onacademictheory,weadaptedthisapproachtoaRapidMultivocalLiteratureReview
(RMLR) protocol.
Rapid Literature Reviews (RLRs) are an agile form of knowledge synthesis, opti-
mized to deliver timely evidence for emerging fields. While traditional Systematic
Literature Reviews (SLRs) emphasize exhaustive coverage, aiming to “identify, eval-
uate and interpret all available research” (Kitchenham 2004), RLRs streamline the
processtocapturekeytrendsandimmediateapplicability (Cartaxoetal.2018;Hamel
et al. 2021; Pizard et al. 2024). Given the rapid growth of Generative AI, a tradi-
tional SLR protocol risks obsolescence before data extraction is even complete. Thus,
an RLR protocol represents a methodologically sound choice to establish a rigorous
baseline of the state of practice. Although this approach may not cover all the steps
of a full SLR, recent empirical assessments suggest that RLRs enable the identifica-
tion of findings in Software Engineering (SE) comparable to those obtained through

AnRMLRextendstheRLRframeworkbyincorporatinggreyliterature(GL)into
its search process, including materials not published in scientific or peer-reviewed
venues,andisoftengroundedinthepracticalexperienceandtechnicalinsightsofpro-
fessionals. The use of grey literature in SE is strongly associated with evidence-based
methods and literature reviews in the field, typically encompassing code repositories,
white papers, and technical documentation, which are commonly identified through
Google Search (Garousi et al. 2016, 2020). As noted by these authors, while GL is
vital for capturing practical insights in emerging fields with strong industry involve-
ment,itintroducesrisksregardingqualityandsourcebias.Toaddressthesechallenges
andensurecredibility,weemployedaqualityappraisalprocedurethatfilteredsources
based on:
1. Authority and Credibility: Prioritizing sources from verifiable organizations or
recognized practitioners over anonymous or unverified content.
2. Technical Evidence: Selecting sources that provide concrete use cases, tool cate-
gorizations, or architectural details, rather than purely opinion-based or high-level
marketing descriptions.
This GL appraisal process ensured that the final selection included only reliable,
evidence-basedsolutions,sourcedfrombothpeer-reviewedpublicationsandgreyliter-
ature, explicitly excluding subjective or purely promotional material. To systematize
the analysis of these diverse sources, this assessment implemented a structured pro-
tocol based on the Rapid Multivocal Literature Review methodology. By adhering
to the meta-protocol defined by (Rocha et al. 2025), we established a rigorous foun-
dation enabled not just the identification but the critical assessment of Generative
AI-basedsolutionsavailableforindustrialapplication.Tooperationalizethisobjective,
the main research question was decomposed into ten specific subquestions, guiding
the systematic extraction and technical characterization of the collected evidence:
• RQ1a. Where can these solutions be found?
• RQ1b. What Generative AI models are used by the solutions?
• RQ1c. How can these solutions be used?
• RQ1d. What technical and operational constraints should be considered when
adopting these solutions?
• RQ1e.Whatarethelimitationsandchallengesfacedbytheidentifiedsolutions?
• RQ1f. What is the maturity level of these solutions?
• RQ1g. Is there technical documentation, support, or an active community that
facilitates their use in academic and industrial contexts?
• RQ1h. What types of users or roles are supported by these solutions?

3.2 Search Strategy and data sources
To identify relevant publications, we designed and executed a search strategy encom-
passing both scientific and grey literature. The specific sources, search strings, and
execution process are detailed below.
Data SourcesForthescientificliterature,weselectedScopusasthesoledatabasefor
study identification, following the approach adopted by Baldassarre et al. (2021) and
Cartaxo et al. (2018), who highlight Scopus is the largest database of peer-reviewed
literature in Software Engineering (SE) and has been widely used in rapid reviews
in this domain. To expand our grey literature search, we used Google Search with

two domain-specific restrictions4 to focus on relevant content from these platforms.
We chose GitHub because it is widely used for sharing and maintaining open-source
software projects, and PapersWithCode for organizing open-access academic articles
related to machine learning.
Search Strings The search strings, presented in Table 5, were systematically con-
structedbasedonthePICOCapproachdetailedpreviouslyinthissection(seeTable2).
A key distinction was made between the Scopus string and the grey literature string.
During initial pilot searches, we observed that Google’s search engine, regardless of
the access method (conventional interface or API), did not reliably process the full,
complex Boolean query used for Scopus. This often resulted in truncated results and
significant loss of evidence, especially when filtering for specific platforms such as
GitHub and PapersWithCode. This limitation is inherent to the search engine’s func-
tionality and is likely related to an internal cap on the number of logical connectors
or search terms that can be included in a single query. To ensure query compatibility
while preserving the core search intent, the grey literature string was reformulated by
removing the ’Outcome’ element from the PICOC structure.
Execution and Refinement The search strings were executed on their respective
platforms. For the scientific literature, the query was run on the Scopus advanced
search interface, accessed via the institutional subscription provided by Brazil’s
CAPES Periodicals Portal5. For the grey literature, the execution was multi-staged.
First, the search string was tested on Google’s standard search interface to validate
the query and ensure the results were compatible and relevant. Once validated, the
Google Custom Search API (Google 2025) was used to extract results into a spread-
sheet for management and analysis systematically. In line with standard practices for
multivocal reviews (Cartaxo et al. 2018), we relied on Google Search’s ranking algo-
rithmandlimitedourinspectiontothefirst100resultsforeachgreyliteraturequery.
Furthermore, we adopted an adapted snowballing strategy tailored for grey literature:
during the review of the retrieved pages, we also explored relevant hyperlinks that
pointedtootherpotentiallypertinentsolutionsnotcapturedbytheinitialsearch.This
additional step aimed to broaden coverage and capture solutions that, although not
directly listed among the top results of the queries, could be relevant to the review’s
inclusion criteria.

Fundamental Limitations
• Data Leakage and Limited Generalizability: A primary limitation is the reliance
onpublicbenchmarkdatasets,whicharesusceptibletodataleakage.AccordingtoHappe
andCito(2023)andKangetal.(2023),popularbenchmarksandsecuritywrite-upswere

during training when evaluating well-known open-source systems. These dependencies
explain why many solutions struggle with cross-project generalization when applied to
proprietary or unseen codebases, where the model cannot rely on memorized patterns.
Li et al. (2023) explains that this difficulty arises because LLMs are often insensitive
to nuances, where subtle differences between similar code sequences can lead to similar
inferences regardless of whether the code is correct or erroneous.
• High False Positive Rates and Limited Explainability:Amajortechnicalbarrier,
particularly in tasks such as vulnerability detection, concerns the frequent generation
of false positives, which undermines developer trust and increases validation overhead.

ticsettings,withmodestprecisionandF1-scoresandatendencytoover-identifypotential

trialsystems.AsidentifiedbyKangetal.(2023),thesetoolsstruggletotrackcorrelations

5.2 Situating the Findings: An Immature but Promising Field
Our findings, while focused on a specific niche of software quality, strongly corroborate and
provide concrete evidence for the broader perception that the application of Generative AI
in Software Engineering remains in its early, experimental stages. Specifically, our empirical
resultsregardingthegeneralizationgapandlackofexplainabilityalignwithrecenttheoretical
concerns raised by Zhou et al. (2025) and Siavvas et al. (2024) regarding the limitations
of LLMs for critical tasks. Broadly state-of-the-art works such as Zhang et al. (2024) and
Schmid et al. (2025) have noted the conceptual nature of many LLM applications in SE.
Ourstudyamplifiesthisbydetailingthisimmaturityspecificallywithinthequalitydomain:
weprovidetangibleevidenceofalandscapedominatedbyacademicprototypes(RQ1f)that
arevalidatedalmostexclusivelyonsyntheticorbenchmarkdatasets(RQ1e),reinforcingthe
significant gap between academic research and practical adoption. The scarcity of industrial
validation identified in our study (RQ1j) is a critical issue that warrants deeper discussion.
We posit several interconnected causes for this phenomenon:
• The “Last Mile” Problem in Academic Research: A significant barrier to the
industrialimpactofacademicresearchiswhatcanbedescribedasthe“lastmileprob-
lem”. This concept, drawn from other fields, symbolizes the inefficiencies in the final
stage of delivering a product or service to the end-user (Li et al. 2025). In our context,
it represents the gap between a functional academic prototype and a robust, usable
tool ready for industrial adoption. Our findings indicate that this gap manifests in two
criticaldimensions.Ontheoperational front,weidentifiedalackoflogisticalreadiness,
evidenced by the scarcity of documentation, user support, and easy-to-use installers.
Simultaneously, on the technical front, many solutions exhibit characteristics that can
limit their actual practicality. As evidenced by Kang et al. (2024) and Li et al. (2023),
performanceonpublicbenchmarksisofteninflatedbydataleakage;consequently,these
prototypesdemonstratepoorcross-projectprediction,failingtogeneralizewhenapplied

vailing black-box nature of these models prevents users from understanding why a
specificfragmentwasflaggedorhowtoremediatetheidentifiedissue(Shengetal.2024).
Consequently, bridging the “last mile” requires a holistic effort, improving engineering
usability while simultaneously solving the algorithmic hurdles of generalizability and
explainability.
• The “Strategic Adoption Dilemma” for Industry: Companies face a significant
dilemma when deciding how to adopt and validate these solutions. On the one hand,
usingpowerful,Closed Models (RQ1b)raisesconcernsaboutdataprivacyandsecurity,
as well as unpredictable costs. This approach introduces a substantial risk of vendor
lock-in, a well-documented challenge in cloud computing where organizations become
dependent on a single provider’s proprietary technology, making future migration diffi-
cult and costly (Opara-Martins et al. 2016). This risk is particularly acute with LLM
APIs,whichoftenlackthepredictableupdatecyclesandformalservice-levelagreements

experimentation and validation.
• Scarcity of Realistic Public Data: A fundamental challenge in empirical software
engineering is that high-quality industrial data, such as comprehensive defect reports
or real-world security vulnerabilities, is often proprietary and highly sensitive. This
scarcity forces many researchers to rely on publicly available benchmarks (as shown in
Table 12) or to use synthetic data, which are artificially generated datasets designed
to emulate original data without revealing sensitive observations (Wang et al. 2025).
While these approaches are helpful for controlled experimentation, they may not accu-
rately reflect the complexity and scale of industrial software systems. This creates a
problematic feedback loop in which academic solutions are not trained on or validated
with industrial-grade data, further widening the gap between research and practice.

In this study, we analyzed four main categories of threats that could contribute to future
replicationattemptsoftheRMLR:internalvalidity,externalvalidity,constructvalidity,and

6.1 Internal Validity
• Threats:The”rapid”natureoftheRMLRimpliedthatnoin-depthassessmentofthe
methodologicalqualityoftheincludedstudieswasconducted.However,itisacommon
practiceintheexecutionofRRs,whichaimtodeliverresultsinashortertimeframe,even
if this implies simplifying traditional steps. As a consequence, technologies developed
andevaluatedwithlowermethodologicalrigormayhavebeenincludedandtreatedwith
thesameweightasthosewithmorerobustmethodologies.Furthermore,theinclusionof
grey literature introduces sources that have not undergone peer review, whose content
may reflect commercial bias or opinions unsupported by solid empirical evidence.
• Mitigations: To mitigate these effects, we acknowledge these limitations as inherent
to the RMLR approach. Our primary mitigation strategy was to design the research
questions themselves to act as a proxy for quality assessment. Specifically, subques-
tions regarding maturity (RQ1f), support (RQ1g), and industrial collaboration (RQ1j)
allowed us to indirectly assess the practical relevance and robustness of each solution’s
evidence. For the grey literature, we adopted a source diversification strategy, consult-
ingmultiplechannels(Google,GitHub,andPapersWithCode)toexpandcoverageand
minimize platform-specific biases.
6.2 External Validity
• Threats: Threats to external validity concern the generalizability of our findings. The
scope of our search was deliberately limited, which introduces four main considera-
tions.First,thesearchinthescientificliteraturewasrestrictedtotheScopusdatabase.
Second, the grey literature analysis considered only the first 100 results from each
queried platform, potentially excluding relevant sources ranked lower by the search
engine. Third, the study included only articles published in English after December
2021, which excludes solutions published in other languages or earlier periods. Finally,
a temporal threat exists due to the rapid evolution of Generative AI, suggesting that
new tools or versions may have emerged between our data collection (April-May 2025)
and publication.
• Mitigations:Theselimitationsweredeliberatescopingdecisions,necessarytoconduct
a“rapid”assessmentfocusedonthemostrecentandaccessiblesolutions.Thisapproach
was justified in the Methodology section, where we emphasized that the objective was
nottobeexhaustivebuttoprovideastructuredoverviewofcurrentpractice.Regarding
the temporal threat, we argue that the critical findings of this assessment, specifically
the”LastMileProblem”andthelackofindustrialreadiness,aresystemiccharacteristics
of the field rather than transient features of specific tools. Therefore, the structural
diagnostic presented here remains a valid and necessary baseline for understanding the
persistent gap between academic prototypes and industrial needs.

6.3 Construct Validity
• Threats: The main threat in this category is related to the operationalization of the
search strings. The concept of ”software quality” is broad and multifaceted, making it
challenging to ensure that all its dimensions are considered when formulating queries.
Furthermore, other terms used, such as those related to Generative AI technologies,
the types of expected solutions, or the application context, may also be ambiguous or
inconsistently used across sources, especially in the grey literature. Additionally, the
need to shorten the search string for Google, imposed by the search engine’s technical
limitations, may have compromised the consistency and scope of data collection across
the different sources.
• Mitigations: To mitigate these threats, we employed a diversified strategy. First, we
usedthePICOCapproachtosystematicallystructureanddecomposeourresearchques-
tionintospecific,searchableterms.Thisprocess,whichincludedtheuseofwildcards(*)
inScopus,helpedcaptureterminologicalvariations.Second,thestringswereiteratively

analysis,wealsoassessedtherelevanthyperlinksonthepagesinitiallyretrieved,allow-
ing us to expand coverage beyond the query’s direct results. While the reduction of
the search string for Google remains a limitation, we argue that this combination of a
structuredapproach(PICOC)andanexploratoryapproach(linkassessment)provided
a robust and transparent data-collection process.
6.4 Conclusion Validity
• Threats: Researcher bias in the selection and classification of studies is an inherent
threat to any review, especially when it involves subjective judgments. The interpreta-
tion of inclusion and exclusion criteria, as well as the categorization of findings, may
vary among team members, potentially affecting the consistency of the results.
• Mitigations: To mitigate this threat, we implemented a rigorous, multi-stage review
protocol throughout the screening process. For the Scopus literature, each stage (title,
abstract, and full text) involved independent evaluations by at least 2 authors. Dis-
agreements were systematically resolved either through a predefined consensus rule, a
third-party mediator, or a final consensus meeting, as detailed in Section 3.3. For the
grey literature, the sources were initially distributed among three authors for indepen-
dent readings. This was followed by a cross-review stage, in which a second author
reassessedeachsource,ensuringthateachitemunderwentadualreviewbeforediscrep-
ancieswerediscussedinaconsensusmeeting.Thisstructured,multi-reviewerapproach,
supported by tools like JabRef for managing scientific literature, was designed to
ensure greater consistency and reliability in the final selection of publications, thereby
strengthening the validity of our conclusions.
7 CONCLUSION
This study aimed to assess the industrial readiness and availability of Generative AI-based
solutions currently available to support quality-related activities in Software Engineering.
Our findings reveal a field in its early stages, characterized by a vibrant academic interest
that has yet to translate into a mature ecosystem of industry-ready tools. The landscape is
markedbyasignificantgapbetweenthestateoftheartinacademicresearchandthestateof

practice in industry, primarily due to a lack of industrial validation, high adoption barriers,
and structural technical limitations regarding model precision and generalizability.
7.1 Implications of this Work
Theresultsofthisstudycarrysignificantimplicationsforbothresearchersandpractitioners.
For researchers, our findings highlight a critical need to bridge the “last mile” gap between
algorithmic prototypes and usable solutions. Future work should prioritize industrial collab-
orations, address the reliability gap through improved explainability and context-awareness,
and validate on real-world data rather than relying solely on synthetic benchmarks, and
createwell-documented,reproducibleartifacts.For practitioners,thisworkservesasaprag-
maticguidetothecurrentlandscape,underscoringthatGenerativeAIshouldbeadoptedas
an assistive technology that augments human expertise, not as a fully autonomous replace-
ment.Practitionersmustcarefullyconsiderthe“strategicadoptiondilemma,”balancingthe
costs and risks of proprietary models against the infrastructure and expertise demands of
open-source alternatives.
7.2 Limitations of this Study
Whileweemployedarigorousmethodology,thisstudyhasinherentlimitationsthatshouldbe
considered.First,ourassessmentreliesonthereportedevidence,documentation,andrepos-
itorydataoftheidentifiedsolutions;wedidnotperformahands-onexecutionorbenchmark
testing of the tools themselves. Second, by focusing on publicly documented solutions, our
search may have missed proprietary, in-house tools used silently within specific companies.
Additionally, while the use of Scopus as the primary academic database provided a broad
baseline,itispossiblethatsomespecifictool-relatedpapersindexedinotherdatabaseswere
not captured, a risk we mitigated by triangulating with extensive grey literature searches.
Finally,regardingtherapidevolutionofGenerativeAI,weacknowledgethatindividualtool
versions may have changed since our data collection. However, as discussed in Section 5,
we contend that the identified structural challenges, such as the “Last Mile Problem”, are
systemic and persist independently of short-term model updates.
7.3 A Roadmap for Future Research
Based on the gaps and opportunities identified, we propose a clear roadmap to advance the
field towards higher quality standards:
1. IndustrialCaseStudiesandBenchmarks:Thereisanurgentneedforin-depthcase
studies evaluating academic prototypes in real-world industrial settings. Furthermore,
althoughchallengingduetoconfidentialityconstraintsandthelimitedpublicavailability
of proprietary systems, the community would greatly benefit from the development
of standardized benchmarks grounded in realistic industrial scenarios. Possible paths
forward include anonymized datasets, curated subsets of proprietary systems under
controlled collaborationagreements, orsystematicallyconstructedbenchmarksderived
from representative open-source repositories (e.g., SWE-bench (Jimenez et al. 2024)).
Such initiatives would enhance the ecological validity, fairness, and reproducibility of
evaluations while strengthening the bridge between academic research and industrial
practice.
2. Quality Guidelines and Standardization: Future research must move beyond ad-
hoc experimentation to establish quality models for GenAI-based SE solutions. This

includesdefiningspecificstandardsforrobustness,explainability,andintegrationreadi-

on specific industrial constraints.
3. ExpandingtheScopeAcrosstheSDLCandISO25010:Asourmappingrevealed

Reliability) to address the full spectrum of systemic quality attributes defined by the
ISO/IEC 25010 standard, such as usability, performance efficiency, and portability.
4. Addressing the “Last Mile” Problem: Research is needed on the software
engineering challenges of transforming GenAI prototypes into robust, industry-ready
tools. This requires a dual approach: (i) addressing operational usability through
IDE integration, human-computer interaction for AI assistants, and seamless CI/CD
deployment; and (ii) solving fundamental reliability hurdles, specifically by enhanc-

AI-generated outputs.
